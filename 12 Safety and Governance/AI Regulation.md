# AI Regulation

Governments worldwide are developing frameworks to govern [[Artificial Intelligence]] development and deployment. The regulatory landscape is fragmented, evolving rapidly, and has significant implications for AI practitioners.

## Major Regulatory Frameworks

### EU AI Act (2024–2026)

The world's first comprehensive AI law, taking full effect in 2026.

**Risk-based classification:**

| Risk Level | Examples | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **Unacceptable** | Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance | Banned |
| **High** | Hiring tools, credit scoring, medical devices, law enforcement | Conformity assessment, transparency, human oversight, documentation |
| **Limited** | Chatbots, deepfake generators | Transparency obligations (disclose AI interaction) |
| **Minimal** | Spam filters, AI in games | No requirements |

**Key obligations for high-risk AI:**
- Risk management system
- Data governance and quality requirements
- Technical documentation
- Record-keeping and logging
- Transparency to users
- Human oversight mechanisms
- Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity

**General-purpose AI models (GPAI):**
- All GPAI must provide technical documentation and comply with copyright law
- "Systemic risk" GPAI (trained with >10²⁵ FLOPs) face additional obligations: adversarial testing, incident reporting, cybersecurity measures

**Penalties:** Up to 7% of global annual turnover for violations.

### US Approach

No comprehensive federal AI law. Instead, a patchwork of:

- **Executive Orders** — Biden's EO 14110 (Oct 2023) on safe AI development; partially rolled back under Trump administration
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** — Voluntary guidelines
- **Sector-specific regulation** — FDA for medical AI, SEC for financial AI, FTC for consumer protection
- **State laws** — Colorado AI Act (2024), California's various proposals
- **Litigation** — Courts shaping rules through copyright and liability cases

### China

- **Algorithmic Recommendation Regulations** (2022) — Transparency for recommendation algorithms
- **Deep Synthesis Provisions** (2023) — Regulating deepfakes
- **Generative AI Measures** (2023) — Providers must register, content must align with "core socialist values"
- **AI safety standards** — Mandatory security assessments for public-facing AI

### UK

- **Pro-innovation approach** — No comprehensive AI law; sector regulators apply existing rules to AI
- **AI Safety Institute** (AISI) — Government body for evaluating frontier AI safety
- **Voluntary commitments** from major AI labs

### Other Notable Jurisdictions

| Country/Region | Approach |
|---|---|
| Canada | AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act) — proposed |
| Brazil | AI regulatory framework under development |
| Japan | Guidelines-based; permissive on training data (see [[AI and Copyright]]) |
| India | Light-touch; focus on AI for development |
| Singapore | Model AI Governance Framework; voluntary, principles-based |

## Key Regulatory Themes

### Transparency
- Disclose when content is AI-generated
- Explain how AI systems make decisions
- Label deepfakes and synthetic media
- Document training data sources

### Safety Testing
- Pre-deployment evaluation of dangerous capabilities
- Red teaming for frontier models
- Incident reporting requirements
- Anthropic's [[AI Safety and Alignment Research#ASL Levels|ASL framework]] as a voluntary example

### Accountability
- Who is liable when AI causes harm?
- The developer? The deployer? The user?
- Product liability frameworks being adapted for AI

### Data Protection
- Training data privacy (GDPR implications)
- Right to erasure vs. inability to "unlearn" from a trained model
- Consent requirements for training data

### Export Controls
- US restrictions on advanced chips to China (see [[AI Hardware and Compute]])
- Controls on sharing model weights with adversarial nations
- Open-source model distribution complications

## Impact on AI Development

### For Model Providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google)
- Documentation and compliance costs
- Safety testing requirements before release
- Potential limits on model scale or capability
- Reporting obligations for incidents

### For Application Developers
- Conformity assessments for high-risk applications
- Transparency requirements (tell users they're talking to AI)
- Record-keeping and audit trails
- Data governance obligations

### For [[AI Coding Harnesses]]
- Code generated by AI may need to be flagged in regulated industries
- [[AI in CI-CD|Automated coding agents]] in financial/medical software may trigger high-risk classification
- Audit trails of AI-generated changes become important
- [[Approval Flows and Sandboxing]] align with regulatory expectations for human oversight

## The Open Source Debate

Should open-source AI models be regulated differently?

**Arguments for exemption:**
- Open source enables innovation, safety research, and democratization
- Regulating weights distribution stifles the ecosystem
- Responsibility should fall on deployers, not model creators

**Arguments for regulation:**
- Open models can be fine-tuned to remove safety guardrails
- Dual-use risks (bioweapons, cyberweapons)
- Once released, models cannot be recalled

The EU AI Act includes partial exemptions for open-source GPAI (except those with systemic risk).
